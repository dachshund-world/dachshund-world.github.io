Dachshund Energy Level: How Much Exercise Do They Really Need?

Dachshunds were built to hunt badgers for hours — they have more stamina than their short legs suggest. Here's exactly how much exercise your sausage dog needs, by age and size.

Miniature & Standard Puppy · Adult · Senior All coat types

u/CyberCat101 via r/Dachshund — on the hunt, exactly what this breed was made for

Quick Answer

Adult dachshunds need 30–60 minutes of exercise per day, split across two walks. Puppies need short 5–10 minute play sessions only. Despite their size, dachshunds have genuine stamina — bred to track badgers underground for hours. Under-exercise is one of the most common reasons owners report problem behaviour.

How energetic are dachshunds?

Dachshunds sit in the moderate-to-high energy category for small breeds. That surprises a lot of first-time owners who expect a lap dog and get a restless, digging, sniffing machine instead. The reason is simple: dachshunds are hounds, not companion breeds. They were selectively bred for centuries to chase badgers into underground tunnels — a job that demands determination, stamina, and a stubborn refusal to quit.

That working heritage doesn't disappear just because your dachshund lives in an apartment. The drive is still there. A dachshund that doesn't get enough exercise will redirect that energy somewhere you don't want it — into your furniture, your shoes, or the far corner of the garden.

That said, dachshunds are also famously good at switching off. After a proper walk they will nap on the sofa for hours without complaint. The key word is after. According to the American Kennel Club, dachshunds are described as curious, friendly, and spunky — traits that only thrive when the dog's physical needs are met.

How much exercise does a dachshund need per day?

The right amount depends on age and size. Here's the practical breakdown:

Adult standard (1–7 yrs)

These are guidelines, not rules. A young standard dachshund who is used to long walks may comfortably do 90 minutes on a hiking trail. An older miniature may prefer three gentle 10-minute outings. Watch your dog, not the clock.

Puppy energy vs adult energy vs senior energy

Dachshund puppies can seem hyperactive — frantic bursts of play followed by sudden, deep sleep. This pattern is normal. What's important is that you don't push a puppy into structured exercise before their growth plates close (around 12 months for miniatures, up to 18 months for standards). Repetitive impact on developing bone can cause lasting joint damage.

Adult dachshunds (1–7 years) hit their peak energy and need the most consistent exercise. This is the phase where you'll notice the consequences of skipped walks most quickly — weight gain, restlessness, or attention-seeking behaviour. The first-time owner guide covers how to build a daily routine that keeps an adult dachshund settled.

Senior dachshunds (8 years and over) often slow down noticeably. They may hesitate before walks they used to love, or lag behind on familiar routes. This is normal aging, not laziness. Shorten walks but maintain the habit — regular gentle movement keeps joints supple and supports a healthy weight into old age. Check the dachshund weight chart to ensure your senior is staying on track as their activity level drops.

Best activities for dachshunds

Not all exercise is equal for a breed with a long spine and short legs. The activities below work with the dachshund's anatomy and instincts rather than against them.

Sniff walks

A sniff walk means letting your dachshund lead — nose to the ground, stopping wherever they want. This is mentally tiring in a way that straight walking isn't. Twenty minutes of sniff walking can settle a dachshund as effectively as a 40-minute brisk walk. It also satisfies the scent-hound instinct directly, which reduces obsessive sniffing or restlessness at home. A well-fitted step-in harness is ideal for sniff walks — it gives control without neck strain when the dog stops and darts unpredictably.

Gentle fetch on soft ground

Short fetch sessions on grass work well for dachshunds. Keep throws low and short — you're not exercising a Labrador. Avoid concrete or gravel surfaces, and never encourage big leaps or repeated jumping to catch a ball.

Puzzle feeders and nose work

On days when weather or your schedule limits outdoor time, puzzle feeders and hidden-treat games provide genuine mental stimulation. Hiding food around the room and letting your dachshund sniff it out activates the same hunting instincts as an outdoor walk. It's not a full substitute, but it helps.

Digging boxes

Dachshunds dig. It's genetic. Rather than fighting it, give them a designated spot — a sandbox or a corner of the garden — where digging is allowed. It burns energy, satisfies instinct, and saves your flower beds.

What to avoid

Some activities that are fine for other breeds carry real risk for dachshunds due to their spinal anatomy:

  • Repetitive stair climbing — occasional stairs are usually fine, but daily stair use as exercise adds cumulative strain. See our guide on whether dachshunds should use stairs.
  • High jumps — jumping on and off furniture or catching frisbees at height puts sudden compressive force on the spine.
  • Forced running on hard surfaces — jogging on pavement for extended periods is hard on short-legged joints.
  • Off-leash running on uneven terrain — a rabbit hole or root underfoot can cause a sudden twisting injury.
Warning: If your dachshund drags their back legs, walks with a hunched posture, yelps when touched along the spine, or suddenly refuses to jump, stop exercise immediately and contact a vet. These can be signs of IVDD — a spinal condition that is common in the breed and requires prompt attention.

Signs your dachshund needs more exercise

Dachshunds are not shy about telling you when their needs aren't being met. Common signs of under-exercise include:

  • Zoomies indoors at odd times — especially evenings
  • Destructive chewing (furniture, shoes, baseboards)
  • Excessive barking or whining without obvious cause
  • Restlessness — unable to settle even after meals
  • Steady weight gain despite no change in food
  • Demanding attention constantly and not responding to "settle"

Most of these resolve within a week of adding a second daily walk. According to Dog Breed Info, dachshunds that get insufficient exercise are more prone to behavioural problems — the breed's stubbornness turns destructive when there's no outlet.

Miniature vs standard dachshund — energy differences

The energy temperament is nearly identical between miniature and standard dachshunds — both are curious, persistent, and driven by scent. The difference is stamina and stride. A miniature dachshund takes more steps per metre and tires faster on distance walks. Miniatures are also slightly more cautious about temperature extremes — a hot pavement that a standard tolerates can overheat a miniature more quickly.

Building a sustainable exercise routine

Consistency matters more than intensity. A dachshund walked 30 minutes every day is healthier and better-behaved than one walked for 2 hours on weekends only. A simple daily structure works well:

  1. Morning walk: 20–30 minutes before breakfast. Sets a calm tone for the day.
  2. Midday sniff session: 10–15 minutes in the garden or yard. Optional but helpful for high-energy individuals.
  3. Evening walk: 20–30 minutes before dinner. Burns the afternoon energy buildup.

On days you can't manage the full routine — rainy days, long work hours — substitute with 10–15 minutes of indoor nose work or a puzzle feeder. Your dachshund will adapt, as long as skipped days are the exception.

Tip: After any walk, give your dachshund 2–3 minutes to sniff freely at the end before heading indoors. This "decompression sniff" helps them wind down properly and reduces the restlessness that sometimes follows exercise when a dog feels mentally unfinished.

Frequently asked questions

Are dachshunds high energy dogs?
Yes, moderately high for their size. Bred as hunting dogs, they have real stamina and a strong need to sniff and explore. They are not naturally calm lap dogs — though they'll happily nap after a proper walk.
How much exercise does a dachshund need per day?
Adults need 30–60 minutes split across two walks. Miniatures can do well with 20–40 minutes. Puppies should only have short play sessions — 5 minutes per month of age, twice a day maximum.
Can I over-exercise my dachshund?
Yes. Repetitive jumping, long runs on hard surfaces, and forced exercise beyond the dog's tolerance can stress the spine and joints. Signs of over-exercise include stiffness after rest, reluctance to walk, and back leg weakness — all need a vet check.
Why is my dachshund so hyper indoors?
Almost always: not enough exercise. Dachshunds redirect hunting energy into zoomies, barking, and chewing if they don't get adequate daily walks. Adding a second walk typically resolves this within a week.
Are miniature dachshunds less energetic than standard?
Slightly. They tire a little faster due to smaller size, but share the same instincts and curiosity. Both need daily exercise — miniatures around 20–40 minutes, standards 45–60 minutes.
What are the best activities for dachshunds?
Sniff walks, gentle fetch on grass, puzzle feeders, nose-work games, and supervised digging. Avoid repetitive stair climbing, high jumps, and long runs on pavement.
How do I know if my dachshund is getting enough exercise?
A well-exercised dachshund settles calmly after walks, maintains a healthy weight, sleeps well, and shows no destructive behaviour. Restlessness, zoomies, and chewing are the main signs of under-exercise.
Do dachshund puppies need less exercise than adults?
Yes. Puppies under 12 months should only do short, gentle play sessions. Their growth plates are still open and long walks can damage developing joints. Stick to the 5-minutes-per-month-of-age rule.
