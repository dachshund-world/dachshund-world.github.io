Dachshund Weight Chart by Age: Miniature & Standard

Month-by-month weight ranges, the rib test for home assessment, and exactly what to do if your dachshund is off track.

Dachshund World Editorial Team  ·  Updated May 2026  ·  12 min read

Why Dachshund Weight Is a Health Issue, Not Just a Number

Most dog owners monitor weight out of general health interest. For dachshund owners, it is more urgent than that. The same gene that gives dachshunds their iconic low-slung build — chondrodysplasia — also predisposes their intervertebral spinal discs to degeneration at a younger age than other breeds. Every extra pound a dachshund carries translates directly into increased mechanical load on those already vulnerable discs.

Research published in veterinary orthopaedics literature consistently links obesity in chondrodysplastic breeds with higher rates of disc herniation and IVDD episodes. This does not mean a slightly-over-weight dachshund will inevitably have a spinal crisis — but it does mean that keeping your dachshund in the healthy weight range is one of the most concrete preventive steps you can take. The weight chart below gives you the numbers; the rest of this guide explains how to use them.

The bottom line: For dachshunds, healthy weight is a spinal health decision as much as a general health decision. A dachshund at ideal body weight has meaningfully better odds of living free from IVDD complications than one carrying excess fat.

Dachshund Size Classifications

Before reading the charts, confirm which classification applies to your dog. The AKC dachshund breed standard recognizes two varieties in the United States based on weight at 12 months of age:

  • Miniature Dachshund: 11 lbs (5 kg) and under at 12 months
  • Standard Dachshund: 16–32 lbs (7–14.5 kg) at full maturity

Dogs between 11 and 16 lbs are informally called "tweenie" dachshunds — not an official AKC category but a widely used term. The weight charts below include tweenie ranges so no dog falls through the cracks.

All three coat types — smooth, long-haired, and wire-haired — occur in both miniature and standard sizes. Coat type does not meaningfully affect weight range within the same variety.

How to Tell If Your Dachshund Is at a Healthy Weight

Weight charts give you a starting point, but body condition is more telling than a number on the scale. A muscular dachshund and a fat dachshund can weigh the same — the number alone tells you nothing about fat distribution. The rib test is the most practical home assessment you can do.

The Rib Test (30-Second Home Check)

Place both hands on your dachshund's sides with fingers pointing down toward the spine. Slide your thumbs along the ribcage, applying gentle pressure — similar to the pressure you'd use to feel a TV remote under a thin blanket.

  • Can feel each rib easily, no pressing required: Healthy weight
  • Have to press firmly before feeling any ribs: Likely overweight
  • Ribs visible and easily felt with no pressure: Likely underweight

The Waist Check (View From Above)

Stand over your dachshund and look straight down. A healthy dachshund should have a visible waist — the body narrows noticeably behind the ribcage before widening at the hips. No waist visible, or a barrel-shaped torso with no narrowing: overweight. A pronounced, sharp narrowing: possibly underweight.

The Tuck Check (Side View)

View your dachshund from the side. The abdomen should angle upward toward the hindquarters — a slight, natural tuck. A pendulous belly with no upward angle strongly suggests excess fat. A very pronounced tuck with the belly drawn up significantly may indicate underweight or an underlying health issue.

Body Condition Score: The Vet's Method

Veterinarians use a standardized 9-point Body Condition Score (BCS) to assess weight independently of the scale — the same system adopted by the World Small Animal Veterinary Association (WSAVA). Understanding the BCS system helps you have a more precise conversation with your vet about your dachshund's weight status.

1–2 Underweight Ribs, spine visible. No fat cover. Significant muscle loss.
3 Thin Ribs easily felt, minimal fat. Waist prominent. Some muscle.
4–5 Ideal ✓ Ribs felt easily, slight fat cover. Waist visible. Tucked abdomen.
6–7 Overweight Ribs felt with pressure. Waist barely visible. Abdomen rounded.
8–9 Obese Ribs not palpable. No waist. Significant fat deposits. Pendulous belly.

For dachshunds specifically, a BCS of 4–5 (out of 9) is the target range. A BCS of 6 or higher meaningfully increases IVDD risk. Ask your vet to score your dachshund at every annual exam and request an explanation if the score changes.

When to Be Concerned About Your Dachshund's Weight

Signs Your Dachshund May Be Overweight

Beyond the rib and waist checks, behavioral and functional signs can indicate excess weight before the scale confirms it:

  • Tires noticeably faster on walks that used to be easy
  • Reluctant to jump up (to couch, car seat) they previously used without hesitation
  • Heavy breathing or panting after mild exertion
  • Visible waddle or rolling gait when walking
  • Snoring in a dog that previously didn't snore

Weight gain is also a potential symptom of hypothyroidism — an underactive thyroid gland that reduces metabolic rate. If your dachshund gains weight despite no change in diet or exercise, request a thyroid panel from your vet before assuming the cause is overfeeding. VCA Hospitals' guide on obesity in dogs explains the health risks and what a weight-loss plan looks like in practice.

IVDD risk context: For dachshunds specifically, being overweight is not just a general health concern — it directly accelerates spinal disc deterioration. Our guide on stairs and dachshund spinal health explains how everyday activities interact with disc disease risk in this breed.

Signs Your Dachshund May Be Underweight

Underweight dachshunds are less common in companion settings but do occur, particularly in rescues or dogs with underlying health conditions:

  • Ribs and spine visible without touching
  • Hip bones prominently visible from above
  • Dull, coarse, or thin coat
  • Low energy and poor muscle tone
  • Poor growth rate in puppies

Before increasing calories in an underweight dachshund, schedule a vet visit. Intestinal parasites, dental pain that makes eating uncomfortable, and metabolic conditions all cause weight loss and require targeted treatment rather than simply more food.

Factors That Affect Dachshund Weight

The weight charts above represent ranges, not fixed targets. Several factors legitimately shift where a specific dog should fall within those ranges:

Neuter Status

Spayed and neutered dachshunds have slower metabolic rates than intact dogs — typically 20–30% lower caloric requirements. Owners who do not adjust food portions after neutering almost invariably see gradual weight gain. The adjustment should happen at the time of the procedure, not after weight gain appears.

Age

Senior dachshunds (8 years and older) typically need 20% fewer calories than they did in prime adulthood, but muscle mass also decreases with age. A senior dachshund may weigh the same as at 5 years but carry significantly more fat and less muscle — making BCS assessment more informative than the scale alone in older dogs.

Activity Level

A dachshund that gets two 30-minute walks daily plus active play burns meaningfully more calories than one that gets a single 10-minute outing. Feeding recommendations on kibble bags assume average activity — highly active dogs need more, sedentary dogs need less.

Diet Type

Raw, home-cooked, and wet food diets have higher moisture content than dry kibble, which affects volume-to-calorie ratios significantly. If you feed a non-standard diet, calorie counting is more important than volume. A veterinary nutritionist consultation is worthwhile for dachshunds on homemade diets.

How to Help an Overweight Dachshund Lose Weight Safely

Target rate: 1–2% of body weight per week. For a 14-lb miniature, that is 0.14–0.28 lbs per week. Faster weight loss in dogs can cause muscle catabolism, gallbladder complications, and nutrient deficiencies.

Step 1: Measure Accurately

Weigh kibble portions in grams using a kitchen scale rather than estimating in cups. Cup volume varies significantly depending on kibble shape and settling — two people "measuring" a cup of the same food can pour meaningfully different calorie amounts. A gram scale eliminates this variable. Weigh your dog monthly on the same scale at the same time of day.

Step 2: Reduce Calories by 10–15%

Do not starve a dachshund into rapid weight loss. Reduce the current daily kibble portion by 10–15% and hold that level for four weeks before reassessing. If weight is dropping at the target rate, maintain the new portion. If not, reduce by another 5–10%.

Step 3: Eliminate Extras

Table scraps, chews, dental sticks, and training treats add up fast. A single dental chew for a miniature dachshund can represent 15–20% of its daily calorie allowance. Switch to using a portion of the daily kibble ration as training treats rather than adding treats on top of meals.

Step 4: Low-Impact Exercise

For overweight dachshunds — especially those with any history of back sensitivity — prioritize low-impact exercise over high-intensity sessions. Swimming (hydrotherapy) is ideal because it burns calories while reducing spinal load. If swimming is not accessible, two 20-minute walks daily on flat ground is a solid starting protocol. A harness that distributes pressure away from the neck is important during these walks; our step-in harness guide covers the best options for the breed's proportions.

Step 5: Prescription Diet If Needed

If standard calorie reduction produces no measurable weight loss after 6 weeks, ask your vet about prescription weight-loss diets (Hill's Metabolic, Royal Canin Satiety Support, Purina OM). These are formulated for higher volume with lower calories, helping dogs feel satisfied while in a calorie deficit. They are available only through veterinary channels for good reason — the nutrient profiles are carefully controlled.

Puppy Weight and Growth Rate

Dachshund puppies grow fastest between 8 weeks and 5 months. During this period, underfeeding to keep a puppy slim is counterproductive — puppies need adequate calories for organ development, bone density, and immune system maturation. Do not calorie-restrict dachshund puppies based on adult weight chart targets. Puppies should be fed to the puppy-specific guidelines on quality puppy food.

If a dachshund puppy is growing faster than the chart ranges suggest, confirm the variety classification first. A puppy consistently above miniature ranges at 3–4 months may simply be a standard or tweenie dachshund — not an overweight miniature. DNA ancestry tests for dogs cannot determine variety; weight progression over several months is the most reliable guide.

When Do Dachshunds Stop Growing?

Most dachshunds reach their full skeletal length and height by 8 months. Body weight — including muscle mass — continues developing until 12–18 months. Standard dachshunds may take slightly longer than miniatures to fully mature. A dachshund at 18 months that has been eating appropriate amounts and exercising regularly is essentially at its adult set point.

Monthly Weigh-In Routine

Building a simple weight-monitoring habit eliminates the guesswork. The two most common methods:

  • Bathroom scale method: Weigh yourself, then weigh yourself holding your dachshund. Subtract. Works well for standard dachshunds. Less accurate for miniatures under 6 lbs where scale precision matters more.
  • Pet scale: A small kitchen or postal scale works for miniature dachshunds. Dedicated pet scales are available from most pet retailers. More accurate for small dogs.

Record readings in a simple note on your phone with the date and the dog's weight. A flat trend line is the goal. A steadily climbing line over 3–4 months is a diet adjustment signal. A sudden, unexplained drop warrants a vet call.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should a miniature dachshund weigh?

A fully grown miniature dachshund should weigh under 11 lbs (5 kg) according to AKC standards. Most healthy miniatures settle between 8 and 11 lbs at 12 months. Anything consistently over 11 lbs warrants a conversation with your vet about weight management.

How much should a standard dachshund weigh?

Standard dachshunds typically weigh between 16 and 32 lbs (7–14.5 kg) at full maturity. Males tend toward the upper end of that range, females toward the lower. A healthy standard dachshund at ideal weight will have ribs you can feel easily with light pressure but not see prominently.

When is a dachshund fully grown?

Most dachshunds reach their full height and length by 8 months but continue to fill out in muscle and body mass until 12–18 months. Standard dachshunds may take slightly longer than miniatures. Weight should stabilize by 18 months.

Is my dachshund overweight?

The most reliable field check is the rib test: run your fingers along your dachshund's side with light pressure. You should feel each rib without pressing hard. If you have to press firmly or cannot feel ribs at all, your dachshund is likely carrying excess weight. The waist should be visible from above and the abdomen slightly tucked when viewed from the side.

Why does dachshund weight matter so much?

Dachshunds carry a genetic predisposition to intervertebral disc disease (IVDD) due to their chondrodysplastic build. Excess body weight places additional mechanical load on already vulnerable spinal discs. Studies suggest obese dachshunds have a significantly higher risk of IVDD episodes than dachshunds maintained at a healthy weight.

How do I help my dachshund lose weight?

The most effective approach is a measured feeding protocol: weigh food portions rather than using cup estimates, eliminate table scraps entirely, use a portion of daily kibble as training treats rather than adding extras, and add a 20–30 minute walk twice daily. Aim for 1–2% body weight loss per week — rapid weight loss in dogs is not healthy.

How much should I feed my miniature dachshund?

Most miniature dachshunds need 1/2 to 3/4 cup of high-quality dry kibble per day, split across two meals. Exact amounts depend on the caloric density of the specific food, your dog's age, and activity level. Always follow the feeding guide on the food packaging and adjust based on your vet's body condition score assessment.

Do male and female dachshunds weigh differently?

Yes, males typically run 10–15% heavier than females within the same variety. A healthy male standard dachshund might weigh 24–32 lbs while a female of the same age and build might be 18–26 lbs. Both fall within the breed standard, so use sex-appropriate ranges when assessing your dog's weight.

Is a tweenie dachshund miniature or standard?

A "tweenie" dachshund is an informal term for dogs that fall between miniature (under 11 lbs) and standard (over 16 lbs) at 12 months of age — typically 11–16 lbs. The AKC does not recognize tweenie as an official classification, but the term is widely used by owners and breeders.

Can dachshunds be underweight?

Yes, and underweight dachshunds have their own health risks including weakened immune function, poor coat quality, and muscle loss. If you can see ribs clearly without touching the dog, or if the spine is prominently visible, the dog is likely underweight. A vet workup is important to rule out parasites, dental pain, or metabolic conditions before increasing calories.
