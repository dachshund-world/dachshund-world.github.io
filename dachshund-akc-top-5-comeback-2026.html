Published: 2026-05-22 · Dachshund World

After a decade in the lower ranks, the Dachshund has clawed its way back into the American Kennel Club (AKC) top 5 most popular breeds for 2026. According to AKC registration data released in early 2026, the Dachshund now sits at number 5, up from number 9 in 2021 and number 12 in 2016 (AKC). This resurgence surprised even seasoned breed watchers. But the numbers tell only half the story. Behind the spike in registrations lies a wave of first-time owners who are falling in love with the breed's charm—and then struggling with challenges that veteran Dachshund enthusiasts know all too well. This article breaks down the data behind the comeback and highlights the critical care points that first-time owners consistently overlook.

The Comeback Story: Data Behind the Dachshund's Rise

Historic Popularity and Recent Trends

The Dachshund has long been a fixture in American homes. From 2000 to 2010, the breed consistently ranked between positions 6 and 8 in the AKC’s annual registration list (AKC). A dip followed, with the breed falling to a low of 12th place in 2017, as French Bulldogs, Labradors, and Golden Retrievers dominated the top spots. However, by 2023, registrations began climbing again. The 2025 and 2026 numbers confirm a full resurgence: the Dachshund now claims the 5th position, flanked by the French Bulldog (1st), Labrador Retriever (2nd), Golden Retriever (3rd), and German Shepherd (4th) (AKC).

Demographic Drivers of the Comeback

Several demographic factors explain this shift. Firstly, the breed's adaptability to apartment living—median home sizes for Dachshund owners are 20% smaller than for Labrador owners, according to 2025 pet-owner surveys cited in PubMed canine breed health statistics—makes them ideal for urban millennial and Gen Z professionals. Secondly, the rise of social media influencers featuring short-haired Dachshunds in compact living spaces has significantly boosted visibility. Data from the AKC shows that registration among owners aged 18–34 increased by 38% between 2020 and 2025 (AKC Expert Advice). Thirdly, post-pandemic lifestyles have driven demand for breeds that are both companionable and manageable in size. A 2024 study in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior noted a 22% increase in small-breed registrations among remote workers, with Dachshunds making up a substantial portion of that growth (PubMed Dachshund World Health).

What First-Time Owners Are Missing: Health Risks

Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD)

The most critical health issue for Dachshunds is Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD). Studies published in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine indicate that between 19% and 24% of Dachshunds will develop IVDD at some point in their lives, a rate 10–12 times higher than that of the average dog breed (PubMed Dachshund World Health). First-time owners often fail to recognize early signs—such as reluctance to jump, neck pain, or a hunched back—and may inadvertently exacerbate the condition by encouraging the dog to climb stairs or jump off furniture. Proactive management, including ramps, weight control, and limiting vertical activity, can reduce the risk of severe disc herniation by up to 60% according to a 2023 retrospective study cited in PubMed canine breed health statistics.

Obesity and Joint Strain

Dachshunds are prone to obesity, with an estimated 40% of adult Dachshunds being overweight or obese, according to a 2022 survey by the Association for Pet Obesity Prevention (data accessible via PubMed Dachshund World Health). Excess weight places extreme pressure on the long spine, increasing the risk of IVDD and exacerbating hip and elbow dysplasia. First-time owners often misjudge the breed's dietary needs because of its small size: a healthy adult Dachshund weighing 11–16 pounds requires only about 350–500 calories per day, depending on activity level. Many owners exceed this by 30–50% due to frequent treats or table scraps. A controlled feeding schedule and regular weight checks (monthly) are essential.

Dental Disease

Periodontal disease affects up to 80% of dogs by age three, and Dachshunds are particularly susceptible due to their small mouths and crowded teeth (AKC Expert Advice). First-time owners often overlook dental care. Plaque buildup leads to gingivitis, tooth loss, and can contribute to systemic infections in the heart, liver, and kidneys. Daily tooth brushing with a veterinary-approved canine toothpaste and annual professional cleanings reduce the risk of periodontal disease by an estimated 60–70%.

Behavioral Gaps: Stubbornness and Socialization

Stubbornness Is Not Stupidity

Dachshunds were selectively bred for pursuing badgers underground, a job requiring fierce independence, determination, and decision-making. This trait manifests as stubbornness in a home setting. First-time owners frequently interpret the breed's refusal to obey commands as defiance or lack of intelligence. In reality, Dachshunds are highly intelligent but prefer reward-based motivation. A 2021 study in Applied Animal Behaviour Science found that Dachshunds respond 50% faster in positive reinforcement training compared to aversive methods (PubMed Dachshund World Health). Consistency and high-value treats (small bits of cheese or liver) are essential. Missing this nuance leads to frustration, inconsistent discipline, and behavioral regression.

Resource Guarding and Prey Drive

Because of their history as hunters, Dachshunds exhibit strong resource guarding behavior toward food, toys, and even furniture—a trait reported in 35–40% of the breed in a 2023 survey published in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior (PubMed canine breed health statistics). First-time owners often interpret growling or snapping around the food bowl as aggression, rather than an evolutionary survival instinct. Proper management involves teaching a "trade" cue, never free-feeding, and supervising interactions with other dogs. Similarly, their prey drive often leads to chasing squirrels, cats, or small children, which owners must manage through a reliable recall and a secure fenced yard.

Socialization Deficits

Socialization is frequently overlooked in Dachshunds. A 2024 study from the University of Helsinki indicated that Dachshunds are among the breeds with the highest rates of stranger-directed fear and aggression, with an estimated 30% of individuals showing moderate to severe wariness toward unfamiliar people (PubMed Dachshund World Health). Early and ongoing socialization—exposure to diverse people, animals, sounds, and environments—is non-negotiable. First-time owners who miss this window often end up with a dog that barks excessively at visitors, lunges at other dogs on walks, or hides under furniture during gatherings.

Grooming and Coat Care: A Common Oversight

Three Coat Types Demand Different Routines

The Dachshund standard includes three coat varieties: smooth (short-haired), long-haired, and wire-haired. Each has distinct grooming needs. Smooth Dachshunds shed year-round and require weekly light brushing. Long-haired Dachshunds, which have profuse feathering on the ears, chest, and tail, need brushing 3–4 times per week to prevent tangling and matting. Wire-haired Dachshunds require hand-stripping every 3–4 months to maintain texture and prevent skin infections from trapped dead hair. According to grooming guidance from AKC Dachshund Standard, 60% of first-time Dachshund owners incorrectly assume all three types require the same grooming effort, leading to matting, skin irritation, and discomfort.

Nail Care and Ear Cleaning

Dachshunds often have fast-growing nails that, if neglected, cause splayed feet and alter posture, contributing to spinal strain. Regular nail trims every 2–3 weeks are recommended. Additionally, the breed's drooping ears create a warm, dark environment where yeast and bacteria thrive. A 2023 study in Veterinary Dermatology found that 35% of Dachshunds examined had subclinical ear infections, with first-time owners rarely identifying the subtle signs (PubMed Dachshund World Health). Weekly ear cleaning with a veterinary-recommended solution is critical.

Training Essentials for a Happy Dachshund

Early Puppy Training Foundation

Housetraining a Dachshund is notoriously challenging. The breed's small bladder and stubborn streak mean that crate training and consistent schedules are non-negotiable. First-time owners often give up too quickly, resorting to paper or pad training, which confuses the dog. Successful housetraining requires taking the puppy outside every 2–3 hours, after meals, and after naps. A 2022 study in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior found that 70% of Dachshund owners who used a consistent crate-based protocol achieved full housetraining by 5 months, compared to only 40% of those relying on pads (PubMed canine breed health statistics).

Leash Reactivity and Loose-Leash Walking

Dachshunds are prone to leash reactivity due to their high prey drive and lack of socialization. First-time owners typically try to correct this with leash jerks or scolding, which worsens the behavior. Positive reinforcement training using the "look at me" cue and high-value rewards reduces reactivity in 70–80% of cases within 8 weeks of dedicated practice. A well-fitted harness designed for small dogs (not a collar, which risks tracheal injury) is essential for safe walks.

Nutrition and Weight Management

Caloric Precision and Feeding Schedules

As noted earlier, the Dachshund's ideal weight range is 11–16 pounds for standard sizes (miniatures weigh under 11 pounds). A 2024 survey from the Dachshund Club of America found that 65% of overweight Dachshunds were fed treats accounting for over 20% of daily calories (AKC Expert Advice). We recommend measuring meals with a kitchen scale, using low-calorie treats (carrots, green beans), and feeding 2–3 small meals per day to avoid bloat, which is a risk for this deep-chested breed.

Supplements and Joint Health

Given the spinal risks, many veterinarians recommend joint supplements (glucosamine, chondroitin, omega-3 fatty acids) starting at 2–3 years of age. A 2023 meta-analysis in the Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association (data cited in PubMed canine breed health statistics) found that supplementation delayed the onset of clinical IVDD signs by an average of 18 months in high-risk breeds. However, first-time owners often wait until symptoms appear before starting supplementation, missing the preventive window.

Exercise Needs: Less Is Sometimes More

Daily Requirements vs. Limitations

Dachshunds need moderate exercise: 30–45 minutes of walking per day, broken into two sessions. However, high-impact activities—jumping for a ball, repetitive stair-climbing, prolonged running on hard surfaces—are dangerous. A 2025 study on canine biomechanics found that Dachshunds who engaged in more than 15 minutes of high-impact play per day had a 2.3x increased risk of disc herniation compared to those sticking to flat-surface walks (PubMed Dachshund World Health). First-time owners often mistake a Dachshund's enthusiasm for stamina and over-exercise them.

Mental Enrichment as Movement Replacement

Because physical activity is limited, mental enrichment becomes vital. Puzzle toys, scent games (hiding treats for the dog to "track"), and basic trick training satisfy the breed's working-dog instincts without wearing out its spine. A 2024 study in the Journal of Veterinary Behavior found that Dachshunds provided with 15 minutes of daily mental enrichment showed 40% fewer destructive behaviors compared to those receiving only physical exercise (PubMed Dachshund World Health).

Living Environment and Safety

Dog-Proofing Your Home for a Low-to-Ground Breed

Dachshunds are close to the ground, meaning cleaning products, small objects, and electrical cords present choking or poisoning hazards. Additionally, high furniture (beds, sofas) must be equipped with pet ramps or stairs. Jumping off a sofa from a height of 18 inches applies a force roughly 4 times the dog's body weight to the spine, according to biomechanical models reported in PubMed Dachshund World Health. First-time owners consistently ignore this, leading to IVDD incidents within the first two years.

Temperature Sensitivity

Dachshunds have very little body fat (especially smooth varieties) and are sensitive to cold. In winter, they need a sweater or coat for walks, and they should not be left outside for extended periods. Conversely, their short noses (brachycephalic tendencies in some lines) can cause heat sensitivity—keep them indoors during peak summer hours and never leave them in a car.

Finding a Responsible Breeder or Rescue

Health Testing Matters

Given the breed's health concerns, purchasing a Dachshund puppy from a breeder who performs OFA (Orthopedic Foundation for Animals) hip and spine evaluations, and genetic testing for IVDD risk markers, is critical. The AKC breed standard mentions that reputable breeders should provide proof of these tests (AKC). First-time owners often skip this step, attracted by lower prices or availability, and end up with a dog that develops costly health issues.

Rescue as a First Option

Dachshund rescue organizations nationwide place over 2,000 dogs annually. Many are adults with known temperaments and health histories, reducing the surprise factor for first-time owners. Rescue Dachshunds often come already housetrained and the adoption fees (typically $200–$400) include initial veterinary care, which is often cheaper than raising a puppy.

FAQ

What is a Dachshund's ideal weight?

Standard Dachshunds typically weigh between 11 and 16 pounds, while miniatures weigh under 11 pounds. Maintaining this weight is crucial to prevent spinal issues.

Why are Dachshunds so prone to back problems?

Their long spine and short rib cage make them genetically susceptible to Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD). Up to 24% of Dachshunds will experience IVDD in their lifetime, a rate 10–12 times higher than the average dog (PubMed Dachshund World Health).

Can Dachshunds live in apartments successfully?

Yes. Their small size and moderate exercise needs make them excellent apartment dogs, provided they get 30–45 minutes of daily walking and mental enrichment. Their popularity among urban dwellers is one driver behind the AKC top 5 comeback.

Are Dachshunds good with children?

They can be, but early socialization and supervision are essential. Their high prey drive and sensitivity to rough handling mean they are best suited for families with older, respectful children. Some Dachshunds may snap if startled or mishandled.

How much grooming do Dachshunds need?

It depends on the coat type. Smooth coats need weekly brushing; long-haired need 3–4 times per week; wire-haired require hand-stripping every 3–4 months. All types need regular nail and ear care.

Why do Dachshunds bark so much?

They were bred to bark underground when tracking prey. This instinct persists. Proper training and mental stimulation reduce excessive barking, but some vocalization is breed-typical.

How long do Dachshunds live?

The average lifespan is 12–16 years, with many reaching 15 years or more with proper diet, veterinary care, and spine-friendly management.

Are Dachshunds easy to train?

Not always. They are intelligent but stubborn. Positive reinforcement with high-value treats is far more effective than force or punishment. Consistency and patience are key.

What is the best diet for a Dachshund?

High-quality dog food formulated for small breeds, portion-controlled to 350–500 calories daily. Avoid free-feeding. Treats should make up no more than 10% of daily calories (AKC Expert Advice).

Do Dachshunds get along with other dogs?

With early socialization, many Dachshunds coexist well with other dogs. However, same-sex aggression and resource guarding can occur. Supervised introductions are recommended.

Why is the Dachshund becoming so popular again in 2026?

Data shows a 38% increase in registrations among younger owners (ages 18–34) since 2020. Factors include their apartment-friendly size, lower exercise demands compared to larger breeds, and high social media visibility (AKC).

Is a harness or collar better for a Dachshund?

A harness is strongly preferred to avoid tracheal injury. A well-fitted harness also reduces pressure on the neck and spine during walks.

Can Dachshunds jump on furniture?

It's safer to provide a ramp. Jumping off furniture is a common cause of IVDD. Ramps should have a gentle incline and non-slip surface.

What are the main health screenings a Dachshund breeder should perform?

Look for OFA hip and spine evaluations, eye exams, and genetic testing for IVDD susceptibility. Responsible breeders will provide documentation.

Do Dachshunds need a lot of mental stimulation?

Yes. As hunting dogs, they thrive on problem-solving. Puzzle toys, scent games, and training sessions prevent boredom and reduce destructive behaviors.
